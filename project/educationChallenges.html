Education Challenges

Digital Inclusion in Education

Digital tools can transform how students learn, but only when everyone can get online. In many parts of the world, learners still lack reliable internet, devices or basic digital skills. Without support, digital learning can deepen, rather than close, existing education gaps.

  • Many schools and homes still have no stable internet connection.
  • Lack of devices and software limits access to digital learning resources.
  • The “digital divide” often reflects deeper inequalities in income, geography and disability.
  • Fair access to technology is essential to make online learning truly inclusive.

Acording to UNESCO a large share of primary and secondary schools around the world are still not connected to the internet. In low-resource contexts, this limits how teachers can use digital platforms, open educational resources and online training. Evidence shows that successful programmes combine investment in broadband infrastructure, affordable devices, teacher training and digital literacy for families, so that technology supports equity instead of creating new barriers.

Gender equality in education

Gender should never decide who gets to learn, yet millions of girls still face barriers to education. Social and economic pressures push many out of school, especially at secondary level. These inequalities affect not only individuals, but whole communities and future generations.

  • Poverty, child marriage and unpaid care work make it harder for girls to stay in school.
  • Many girls face unsafe learning environments and gender-based violence.
  • Girls in conflict or crisis-affected areas are at even greater risk of being out of school.
  • Educating girls is linked to better health, higher incomes and stronger participation in society.

Access to quality education
for all

Being enrolled in school does not always mean receiving a quality education. Many children still have no access at all, while others sit in overcrowded classrooms with too few trained teachers and limited learning materials.

  • Hundreds of millions of children and young people remain out of school.
  • Overcrowded classes and a lack of trained teachers limit effective learning.
  • Many students do not master basic reading and numeracy skills on time.
  • Poor-quality education reduces future opportunities and keeps families in poverty.

The World Bank describes a crisis of “learning poverty”, where a high share of 10-year-olds in low- and middle-income countries cannot read and understand a simple text. Research highlights the need for coordinated reforms: investment in teacher training, early-grade reading programmes, inclusive curricula, targeted support for disadvantaged learners and safe school infrastructure. Improving both access and quality together is essential to break cycles of disadvantage over time.

Eco-conscious development
in education

Education plays a vital role in preparing learners to respond to the climate and environmental crisis. Yet sustainability is still missing or very limited in many school systems, leaving students without tools to understand these changes.

  • Many national curricula barely mention climate change or biodiversity.
  • Teachers often feel unprepared to teach environmental topics confidently.
  • Schools can become models of sustainability through projects and daily practices.
  • Environmental education helps young people make informed, responsible choices.

Recent analyses by UNESCO show that around half of national curriculum frameworks reviewed make no reference to climate change at all. To address this gap, UNESCO’s Education for Sustainable Development (ESD for 2030) framework encourages countries to “green” their education systems: integrating climate and environmental topics across subjects, supporting teachers with training and resources, and involving students in real-world projects such as school gardens, recycling schemes and energy-saving initiatives.
